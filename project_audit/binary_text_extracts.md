2-3 broad keywords (e.g., "vehicle safety", "road accidents")

Chapter 1
INTRODUCTION
This chapter puts emphasis on the basic terminologies and the introductory concepts in graph matching. The chapter describes the basic definition of graph, types of graph that applies to the context, different types of graph matching methods and how graph matching can be used for matching images.
Information about Project
A graph is a representation of a set of objects where some pairs of objects are connected by links. The interconnected objects are represented by mathematical abstractions called vertices, and the links that connect some pairs of vertices are called edges.Typically, a graph is depicted in diagrammatic form as a set of dots for the vertices, joined by lines or curves for the edges.
A graph G = (V,E) in its basic form is composed of vertices and edges. V is the set of vertices(also called nodes or points) and E ⊆V × V (also known as arcs or lines) of graph G.
In this dissertation, a graph is considered as a 4-tuple G = (V, E, A,B), where
• V is the finite set of vertices.
• E ⊆V × V is the set of edges.
• A: V → L is a function assigning labels to the vertices.
• B: E → L is a function assigning labels to the edges.

1.1.1 Labelled Graph
Graphs with labels attached to edges or vertices are more generally designated as labelled. The vertices of a graph, by their nature as elements of a set, are distinguishable. This kind of graph may be called vertex-labelled. Graphs with labelled edges are called edge-labelled graphs.Consequently, graphs in which vertices are indistinguishable and edges are indistinguishable are called unlabelled graph.
1.1.2 Attributed Relational Graph
An Attributed Relational Graph (ARG) is a graph where both the nodes and the edges are representedwith attributes. Node attributes describes the nodes by representing quantities such as shape, position, size and colour of an object whereas edge attributes define relationships between nodes like the distance between two points or dissimilarity between objects.
Information about Industry
Graph matching is the process of comparing two graphs, in which the nodes and edges of one graph are mapped to the nodes and edges of another graph, to find an appropriate correspondence between the two graphs. The mapping of one graph to another must satisfy some constraints or optimality criteria,ensuring that similar substructures in one graph are mapped to similar substructures in the other.
Graph matching methods can be divided into two broad categories [1]:
Exact matching
Inexact matching
1.2.1 Exact Matching
Exact matching method finds a strict correspondence between two graphs. In this method, the mapping between thenodes of the two graphs must be edge-preserving in the sense that if two nodesin the first graph are linked by an edge, they are mapped to two nodes in thesecond graph that are linked by an edge as well.For ARGs, the matching ensures that the attributes are also identical in both graphs.
Exact matching problems can be divided into following types [2]:
Graph Isomorphism
It is the most stringent form of exact matching, in which an exact structural correspondence must exist between the two graphs and the mapping must be bijective. That is, a one-to-one correspondence must be found between each node of the first graph and each node of the second graph. Example of graph isomorphism is shown in Figure 1.3.

Subgraph Isomorphism:
It is a weaker form of matching. If nodes along with their corresponding edges are deleted from a graph G, a sub graph G′ denoted by G′ ⊆G is obtained. A sub graph isomorphism from G to G′ is an isomorphism from a graph G′ to a sub graph G′ of G. An example of subgraph isomorphism is shown in Figure 1.4.
1.2.1.3Monomorphism
It is a more relaxed type of matching than subgraph isomorphism in which, the nodes and edges of the first graph are mapped to the nodes and edges of the second graph; the second graph, however, may have both extra nodes and extra edges. Example of graph monomorphism is shown in Figure 1.5.
Homomorphism
It is a still weaker form of matching, which drops the condition that nodes in the first graph are to be mapped to distinct nodes of the other; hence, the correspondence can be many-to-one.
Maximum Common Subgraph(MCS)
An MCS of two graphs, G and G′, is a graph G″ that is a sub graph of both G and G′, such that it has the maximum number of nodes among all possible sub graphs of G and G′ i.e. itmaps a sub graph of the first graph to an isomorphic sub graph of the second one. The goal of the algorithm is to find the largest sub graph for which such a mapping exists. It can be used to measure the similarity of objects as, the larger the MCS, more will be the similarity.
